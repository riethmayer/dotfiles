# The 15 Smart Brevity Rules

Ordered by impact. Each rule has the same shape: what, why, how to verify, a clear example pair.

When tagging a block with `rules_violated`, use the rule number. Pick the 1–3 most load-bearing violations, not every rule that technically applies.

---

## 1. Lead with the answer

**Rule:** Put the conclusion first.
**Why:** Readers decide in seconds whether to continue.
**Verification:** A reader should understand the core message from the first sentence alone.
**Good:** "Revenue grew 38% because enterprise expansion accelerated."
**Bad:** "Over the last several quarters we've observed a number of interesting developments across the business."

---

## 2. One idea per sentence

**Rule:** Avoid stacking concepts.
**Why:** Cognitive load destroys retention.
**Verification:** Each sentence expresses one primary thought.
**Good:** "Hiring slowed in Q2."
**Bad:** "Hiring slowed in Q2 while retention improved and product velocity increased despite infrastructure migration."

---

## 3. Delete all non-essential words

**Rule:** Remove filler, hedging, repetition.
**Why:** Density reduces clarity.
**Verification:** Removing a word should not change meaning.
**Good:** "Margins improved 12%."
**Bad:** "Margins actually improved by approximately 12% overall."

---

## 4. Explain why it matters

**Rule:** Translate facts into implications.
**Why:** Relevance drives attention.
**Verification:** Every key fact answers "so what?"
**Good:** "Churn fell 3%, extending runway by six months."
**Bad:** "Churn fell 3%."

Use an explicit `**Why it matters:**` line for memos, updates, and long docs. Skip for one-line Slack replies.

---

## 5. Structure for scanning

**Rule:** Use headers, bullets, whitespace, bolding.
**Why:** Most readers skim.
**Verification:** A scanner extracts the key points in under 10 seconds.
**Good:** Short sections, bullets, bolded decisions.
**Bad:** Dense 20-line paragraphs.

---

## 6. Put the highest-value information first

**Rule:** Front-load insight, stakes, and actions.
**Why:** Readers may never reach the end.
**Verification:** The first third contains the most important information.
**Good:** "Customer growth stalled after pricing changes."
**Bad:** Saving the key insight for the conclusion.

---

## 7. Prefer simple words

**Rule:** Use plain English over jargon.
**Why:** Simplicity increases trust and speed.
**Verification:** Replace abstract words with simpler equivalents.
**Good:** "We cut costs."
**Bad:** "We executed efficiency optimization initiatives."

---

## 8. Use short paragraphs

**Rule:** Keep visual blocks small.
**Why:** Large blocks repel readers.
**Verification:** Most paragraphs stay below 3–4 lines.
**Good:** Two-sentence paragraphs.
**Bad:** Half-page walls of text.

---

## 9. Use strong verbs

**Rule:** Replace abstract phrasing with action.
**Why:** Verbs create energy and precision.
**Verification:** Hunt weak verbs — "is", "has", "does", any "-ing" passive.
**Good:** "The team launched the feature."
**Bad:** "The feature was in the process of being launched."

---

## 10. Make headlines informative

**Rule:** Titles communicate value independently.
**Why:** Headlines determine engagement.
**Verification:** A reader understands the core takeaway from the title alone.
**Good:** "AI Copilot cut analyst research time 42%."
**Bad:** "Quarterly Update."

---

## 11. Bold selectively

**Rule:** Highlight only the load-bearing 1–3 words per block.
**Why:** Visual hierarchy guides scanning. Universal bolding is no bolding.
**Verification:** Bolding identifies only the few most important words.
**Good:** "Decision: **Delay launch by 2 weeks.**"
**Bad:** Entire paragraphs in bold.

---

## 12. Remove warm-up sentences

**Rule:** Start immediately with substance.
**Why:** Delay loses attention.
**Verification:** Deleting the first sentence should not improve the text. If it does, delete it.
**Good:** "The fund closed at €350M."
**Bad:** "As many of you know, we've been working hard over recent months."

Common offenders: "I wanted to reach out", "Hope this finds you well", "As discussed", "Just following up", "I hope you're doing well".

---

## 13. Write conversationally

**Rule:** Sound human, not institutional.
**Why:** Human tone lowers friction.
**Verification:** Read aloud. If it sounds robotic, rewrite.
**Good:** "Customers ignored the feature."
**Bad:** "The feature experienced suboptimal user engagement dynamics."

---

## 14. Compress after drafting

**Rule:** Editing is mandatory.
**Why:** First drafts are rarely efficient.
**Verification:** Final is 20–30%+ shorter than draft one with same meaning.
**Good:** 120-word memo reduced to 80 words with same meaning.
**Bad:** Shipping draft one unchanged.

For this skill: the "after" should be measurably shorter unless the source is already tight. Aim for 30–60% reduction in most cases.

---

## 15. Optimize for clarity, not completeness

**Rule:** Prioritize understanding over exhaustive detail.
**Why:** Communication succeeds only if absorbed.
**Verification:** A reader recalls the main point after one read.
**Good:** "Three priorities: hiring, retention, expansion."
**Bad:** Listing every operational detail.

---

# Pre-ship checklist

Before declaring a rewrite done:

1. Can the first sentence stand alone? (Rule 1)
2. Can any sentence be deleted? (Rule 3)
3. Is the "why it matters" explicit where it should be? (Rule 4)
4. Can a scanner understand it in 10 seconds? (Rule 5, 6)
5. Did formatting actually improve readability, not decorate it? (Rule 5, 11)
6. Did any jargon survive unnecessarily? (Rule 7)
7. Are the key actions or decisions obvious?
8. Is the headline specific? (Rule 10)
9. Does every paragraph earn attention? (Rules 1–15 cumulative)
10. Can it be cut by another 20%? (Rule 14)
