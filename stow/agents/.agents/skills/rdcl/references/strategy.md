Based on the RDCL Toolkit v3.1.0 (CC BY-SA 4.0) by radicalproduct.com.

Strategy has three sequential sub-steps:

## Step 2a: Sustainability Analysis

### Why sustainability matters
In addition to the right Vision, you need to consider what factors influence your Sustainability — so you survive long enough to get there. "Do you have enough food and water to survive this journey? Do you have a crew? Is your boat structurally sound?"

### Risk Survey
Identify risks across 5 categories:

| Category | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Technology Risk** | Risk of failing to develop new technology or operational systems critical to your product's key value proposition |
| **Legal/Regulatory Risk** | Risk of being shut down or prevented from operating due to legal injunction or regulatory issues |
| **Financial Risk** | Risk of running out of liquid cash to pay employees and suppliers |
| **Personnel Risk** | Risk that you are unable to hire or retain key personnel |
| **Stakeholder Risk** | Risk that an influential stakeholder (e.g. a sponsoring executive) decides to shut down further work |

### Sustainability Statement
After identifying the top 1-2 risks, fill in:

```
Currently, the greatest risk to our product's existence is that
    ______________________________ (greatest risk)

If this happens, we won't be able to continue operating because
    ______________________________ (consequence(s) of risk)

This risk will most likely come true if
    ______________________________ (factors that increase/amplify risk)

Some factors that could help us mitigate the risk are
    ______________________________ (factors that decrease/mitigate risk)
```

## Step 2b: RDCL Strategy Canvas

Generate at least 2-3 different strategic approaches. Each strategy defines all four RDCL components:

### R — Real Pain Points
Validated problems or desires that your product addresses. What real, validated problems will your product solve for users/customers?

### D — Design (Interface + Identity)
Two aspects:
- **Interface** — how the product will be used (user-facing features, exposed functionality)
- **Identity** — how the product will be perceived (tone, look and feel, brand)

Research shows that matching your product's identity to users' desires and expectations creates connection that can outweigh usability problems and other functional limitations.

### C — Capabilities
How you deliver on the promises made by your Design. What technology, expertise, data, partnerships, or other capabilities must you develop? Ideally these should be **unique and hard-to-copy**.

### L — Logistics
How the product gets into customers' hands. Pricing, delivery, installation, support, subscription/upgrade model.

### The flow between RDCL elements

```
REAL PAIN POINTS ←→ LOGISTICS
    ↕ SUPPORT/USAGE        ↕ (last mile: pricing, delivery, support)
DESIGN ←→
    ↕ DELIVERY/FEATURES
CAPABILITIES ←→
    ↕ OPERATIONS
```

### Important checks
- Try at least 2-3 different combinations of R, D, C, and L
- Before proceeding, verify no internal contradictions (e.g. a Design element based on a Capability you've decided not to develop)

## Step 2c: Vision/Sustainability Test

Evaluate each strategy option on a 2x2 matrix:

```
                    GOOD VISION FIT
                         |
  VISION INVESTMENT      |      IDEAL
  Good vision fit, but   |  Good vision fit AND
  unsustainable. Be      |  sustainable. If most items
  careful!               |  land here, you've found
                         |  a winning strategy.
  ─────────────────────────────────────────
  WORSENS                |              IMPROVES
  SUSTAINABILITY         |          SUSTAINABILITY
  ─────────────────────────────────────────
  DANGER!                |      VISION DEBT
  Poor vision fit AND    |  Poor vision fit, but
  unsustainable. Only    |  sustainable. Items here
  accept if they unlock  |  represent vision debt that
  future opportunities.  |  must eventually be paid back.
                         |
                    POOR VISION FIT
```

### How to evaluate each R, D, C, L item:
- **Vision Fit**: Would doing this move you closer to your vision, or further away?
- **Sustainability**: Would doing this mitigate your risks (from the Sustainability Statement), or amplify them?

### Picking a strategy
Choose the strategy with the best balance of vision fit and sustainability. Most items should land in the "Ideal" quadrant. There is no simple formula — gather input from your organization, weigh your options, and make the call.
